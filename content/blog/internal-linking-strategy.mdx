Internal linking is the fastest SEO lever most sites already control.

I do not say that because internal links are glamorous. They are not. I say it because when I audit a site with 20 to 200 pages, the same pattern appears again and again: the owner keeps publishing, but the pages that should rank are buried, orphaned, or linked with anchors that say nothing.

My internal linking strategy is simple: **route authority from pages Google already trusts to pages the business needs to grow.**

## The short version

Internal links do three jobs:

| Job | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Discovery | Google finds URLs through links |
| Context | Anchor text helps explain what the target page is about |
| Priority | Important pages should receive stronger, more relevant links |

Google's link documentation says links help it find pages and understand relevance, and that good anchor text should be descriptive and relevant: [Google link best practices](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/links-crawlable).

That is the foundation. The operator work is deciding which links to add first.

## What competitors do well

The internal linking SERP is dominated by pages from brands like Yoast, Semrush, Backlinko, Search Engine Land, and technical SEO shops. The winning pattern is clear:

- They define internal links quickly.
- They explain crawl discovery and authority flow.
- They cover orphan pages.
- They include anchor text rules.
- They show pillar-cluster thinking.
- They turn the topic into a repeatable audit workflow.

Yoast frames internal linking as a way to pass strength from high-authority or popular posts to newer or less visible pages. Semrush emphasizes orphan pages and site structure. Backlinko breaks the basics down cleanly for beginners.

My angle is more operational: where do I add links this week to move rankings, crawl depth, and conversions?

## My internal link audit

I start with four exports:

1. GSC pages with impressions.
2. A crawl with internal link counts and depth.
3. Sitemap URLs.
4. Blog/category inventory.

Then I tag each page:

| Page type | What I do |
|---|---|
| High impressions, low position | Add links from relevant pages that already have traffic |
| High impressions, low CTR | Improve snippet and add support links |
| Indexed but no impressions | Link from the closest hub or reconsider intent |
| Orphan page | Add contextual links or noindex if not useful |
| Commercial page | Link from informational posts with buying intent |
| Pillar page | Link from every relevant spoke |

This is where internal linking becomes strategy instead of decoration.

## The donor and acceptor model

I call source pages "donors" and target pages "acceptors."

A good donor page has:

- Existing impressions or clicks
- Topical relevance
- Crawlable links
- Enough body content for contextual anchors
- A reason for the reader to continue

A good acceptor page has:

- Business value
- Ranking potential
- Clear intent
- Enough content quality to deserve the link

Do not send every link to the homepage. That is lazy routing. Send links to the page that best answers the next question.

## Anchor text rules I actually use

I avoid exact-match spam, but I also avoid useless anchors.

| Weak anchor | Better anchor |
|---|---|
| click here | technical SEO checklist |
| read more | why your content is not ranking |
| this guide | Google core update recovery workflow |
| our blog | AI SEO and GEO blog |

I vary anchors naturally:

- "internal linking strategy"
- "fix orphan pages"
- "route authority to priority pages"
- "topic cluster links"

The point is clarity, not repetition.

## Pillar-cluster linking

For SERP Strategists, I want the content cluster to behave like a map.

Example:

| Pillar | Supporting pages |
|---|---|
| AI SEO automation | AI SEO agent vs agency, AI SEO tools pricing, agentic web SEO |
| GEO and AI search | GEO guide, Google AI Overviews, Perplexity SEO, AI visibility measurement |
| Content recovery | Why content is not ranking, E-E-A-T, core update recovery, cannibalization |
| Technical SEO | Core Web Vitals, schema markup, technical checklist, internal linking |

Every supporting page should link back to the closest pillar. Pillars should link out to the most useful spokes. Related spokes should link sideways only when the reader genuinely benefits.

## Low-hanging GSC workflow

When I want quick movement, I do this:

1. Export pages ranking in positions 10-30.
2. Pick five URLs with business relevance.
3. Find three donor pages for each.
4. Add descriptive contextual links.
5. Update related sections if the link feels forced.
6. Recheck GSC after recrawl.

This often works faster than writing a new article because Google already knows the target page exists.

## The link placement test

I do not add a link unless it passes three tests:

| Test | Question |
|---|---|
| Reader fit | Would a reader naturally want this next? |
| Topic fit | Does the source page create context for the target page? |
| Business fit | Does the target page support authority, conversion, or recovery? |

If a link only passes the SEO test but fails the reader test, I usually skip it. Forced links weaken the page and make the article feel engineered instead of helpful.

## A simple anchor distribution model

For an important page, I want anchor variety:

| Anchor type | Example |
|---|---|
| Exact topic | internal linking strategy |
| Problem-led | fix orphan pages |
| Outcome-led | improve crawl depth |
| Workflow-led | internal link audit |
| Branded/contextual | SERP Strategists technical SEO workflow |

This gives Google enough clarity without making the site look mechanically optimized.

## What not to do

Do not add sitewide footer links to every blog post and call it strategy.

Avoid:

- Too many links in one paragraph
- Same anchor repeated everywhere
- Links that interrupt the reader
- Linking only to recent posts
- Linking to weak pages just because they exist
- Navigation bloat

Internal links are recommendations. If every page recommends every other page, the signal becomes weak.

## Internal linking for GEO

Internal links also help AI systems understand entity relationships.

If a page about "zero-click search" links to "AI Overviews," "GEO," and "AI search visibility," the site is building a semantic graph. That graph helps both crawlers and readers understand how concepts fit together.

That is why I think about internal links as:

- Crawl paths
- Authority paths
- Reader paths
- Entity paths

SEO and GEO both benefit when those paths are clean.

## My weekly link maintenance routine

Every week, I would check:

| Check | Why |
|---|---|
| New posts with fewer than 3 internal links | Prevent orphaning |
| Pages in positions 10-30 | Find quick-win acceptors |
| High-traffic posts with stale CTAs | Route users to current offers |
| Broken internal links | Remove dead crawl paths |
| Cannibalized pages | Consolidate or clarify links |

This takes less time than writing a new post and often has more immediate impact.

## The final rule

Internal linking is not about adding more links. It is about making the site explain itself.

If Google, AI systems, and readers can all follow the same path from question to answer to next action, the site becomes easier to rank and easier to convert.

That is the entire game.
